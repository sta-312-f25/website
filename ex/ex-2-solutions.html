Execise 1 Solutions

Problem 1

Given matrices: \[\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & -2 \\ 4 & 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{C} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ 2 & 6 \end{bmatrix}\]

a) Dimensions of each matrix: - \(\mathbf{A}\): \(2 \times 3\) (2 rows, 3 columns) - \(\mathbf{B}\): \(3 \times 2\) (3 rows, 2 columns)
- \(\mathbf{C}\): \(2 \times 2\) (2 rows, 2 columns)

b) Which operations are possible: - \(\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}\): Not possible (different dimensions: \(2 \times 3\) vs \(3 \times 2\)) - \(\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}\): Possible (inner dimensions match: \(2 \times 3\) and \(3 \times 2\)). Result: \(2 \times 2\) - \(\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}\): Possible (inner dimensions match: \(3 \times 2\) and \(2 \times 2\)). Result: \(3 \times 2\) - \(\mathbf{C} \times \mathbf{B}\): Not possible (inner dimensions don’t match: \(2 \times 2\) and \(3 \times 2\))

c) Calculate \(\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}\):

\[\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & -2 \\ 4 & 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \times \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\]

Element-by-element calculation: - \((1,1)\): \((1)(2) + (3)(-1) + (-2)(0) = 2 - 3 + 0 = -1\) - \((1,2)\): \((1)(1) + (3)(3) + (-2)(4) = 1 + 9 - 8 = 2\) - \((2,1)\): \((4)(2) + (0)(-1) + (5)(0) = 8 + 0 + 0 = 8\) - \((2,2)\): \((4)(1) + (0)(3) + (5)(4) = 4 + 0 + 20 = 24\)

Therefore: \(\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 2 \\ 8 & 24 \end{bmatrix}\)

Problem 2

Given \(\mathbf{P} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 4 \\ 3 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{Q} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ -1 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\)

a) Find transposes:

\[\mathbf{P}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ -1 & 0 \\ 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{Q}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 0 \\ 2 & 3 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\]

b) Calculate \(\mathbf{P} \times \mathbf{Q}\):

\[\mathbf{P} \times \mathbf{Q} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 4 \\ 3 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \times \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ -1 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\]

  • \((1,1)\): \((2)(1) + (-1)(-1) + (4)(0) = 2 + 1 + 0 = 3\)
  • \((1,2)\): \((2)(2) + (-1)(3) + (4)(1) = 4 - 3 + 4 = 5\)
  • \((2,1)\): \((3)(1) + (0)(-1) + (1)(0) = 3 + 0 + 0 = 3\)
  • \((2,2)\): \((3)(2) + (0)(3) + (1)(1) = 6 + 0 + 1 = 7\)

Therefore: \(\mathbf{P} \times \mathbf{Q} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 5 \\ 3 & 7 \end{bmatrix}\)

c) Calculate \((\mathbf{P} \times \mathbf{Q})^T\):

\[(\mathbf{P} \times \mathbf{Q})^T = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 5 \\ 3 & 7 \end{bmatrix}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 3 \\ 5 & 7 \end{bmatrix}\]

d) Calculate \(\mathbf{Q}^T \times \mathbf{P}^T\) and verify:

\[\mathbf{Q}^T \times \mathbf{P}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 0 \\ 2 & 3 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \times \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ -1 & 0 \\ 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\]

  • \((1,1)\): \((1)(2) + (-1)(-1) + (0)(4) = 2 + 1 + 0 = 3\)
  • \((1,2)\): \((1)(3) + (-1)(0) + (0)(1) = 3 + 0 + 0 = 3\)
  • \((2,1)\): \((2)(2) + (3)(-1) + (1)(4) = 4 - 3 + 4 = 5\)
  • \((2,2)\): \((2)(3) + (3)(0) + (1)(1) = 6 + 0 + 1 = 7\)

Therefore: \(\mathbf{Q}^T \times \mathbf{P}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 3 \\ 5 & 7 \end{bmatrix}\)

Verification: \((\mathbf{P} \times \mathbf{Q})^T = \mathbf{Q}^T \times \mathbf{P}^T\) ✓

Problem 3

a) Find the inverse of \(\mathbf{R} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\):

For a \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\), the inverse is \(\frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix}\)

First, find the determinant: \(\det(\mathbf{R}) = (3)(1) - (1)(2) = 3 - 2 = 1\)

Since \(\det(\mathbf{R}) \neq 0\), the inverse exists:

\[\mathbf{R}^{-1} = \frac{1}{1}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -2 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\]

b) Verify \(\mathbf{R} \times \mathbf{R}^{-1} = \mathbf{I}\):

\[\mathbf{R} \times \mathbf{R}^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \times \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\]

  • \((1,1)\): \((3)(1) + (1)(-2) = 3 - 2 = 1\)
  • \((1,2)\): \((3)(-1) + (1)(3) = -3 + 3 = 0\)
  • \((2,1)\): \((2)(1) + (1)(-2) = 2 - 2 = 0\)
  • \((2,2)\): \((2)(-1) + (1)(3) = -2 + 3 = 1\)

Therefore: \(\mathbf{R} \times \mathbf{R}^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{I}\) ✓

c) Why \(\mathbf{S} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\) has no inverse:

\(\det(\mathbf{S}) = (2)(2) - (4)(1) = 4 - 4 = 0\)

Since the determinant is zero, the matrix is singular (non-invertible). This occurs because the second row is exactly twice the first row, making the rows linearly dependent.

Problem 4

a) Find \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{x})\) where \(\mathbf{a} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\):

The derivative rule states: \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{a}\)

Therefore: \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{x}) = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\)

b) For \(f(\mathbf{x}) = 2x_1 - 5x_2 + 3x_3\):

We can write this as \(\mathbf{c}^T\mathbf{x}\) where \(\mathbf{c} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -5 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix}\)

Using the same rule: \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{c} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -5 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\)

Problem 5

Given \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix}\)

a) Expand \(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}\) into scalar form:

First, calculate \(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}\): \[\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2x_1 + 3x_2 \\ x_1 + 4x_2 \end{bmatrix}\]

Then calculate \(\mathbf{x}^T(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x})\): \[\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 & x_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2x_1 + 3x_2 \\ x_1 + 4x_2 \end{bmatrix}\]

\[= x_1(2x_1 + 3x_2) + x_2(x_1 + 4x_2) = 2x_1^2 + 3x_1x_2 + x_1x_2 + 4x_2^2\]

\[= 2x_1^2 + 4x_1x_2 + 4x_2^2\]

b) Find \(\mathbf{A}^T\) and \(\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{A}^T\):

\[\mathbf{A}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\]

\[\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{A}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 4 \\ 4 & 8 \end{bmatrix}\]

c) Find \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x})\) using the derivative rule:

The rule states: \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}) = (\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{A}^T)\mathbf{x}\)

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}) = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 4 \\ 4 & 8 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4x_1 + 4x_2 \\ 4x_1 + 8x_2 \end{bmatrix}\]

Problem 6

Given \(\mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -1 & 2 \\ -1 & 3 & 0 \\ 2 & 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix}\)

a) Verify that \(\mathbf{B}\) is symmetric:

A matrix is symmetric if \(\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{B}^T\). Check that \(b_{ij} = b_{ji}\) for all \(i,j\): - \(b_{12} = -1 = b_{21}\) ✓ - \(b_{13} = 2 = b_{31}\) ✓
- \(b_{23} = 0 = b_{32}\) ✓

Therefore, \(\mathbf{B}\) is symmetric.

b) Find \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{B}\mathbf{x})\) using the symmetric matrix rule:

For symmetric matrices: \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{B}\mathbf{x}) = 2\mathbf{B}\mathbf{x}\)

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{B}\mathbf{x}) = 2\begin{bmatrix} 4 & -1 & 2 \\ -1 & 3 & 0 \\ 2 & 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix} = 2\begin{bmatrix} 4x_1 - x_2 + 2x_3 \\ -x_1 + 3x_2 \\ 2x_1 + 5x_3 \end{bmatrix}\]

\[= \begin{bmatrix} 8x_1 - 2x_2 + 4x_3 \\ -2x_1 + 6x_2 \\ 4x_1 + 10x_3 \end{bmatrix}\]

c) Using the general rule \((\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{A}^T)\mathbf{x}\):

Since \(\mathbf{B}\) is symmetric, \(\mathbf{B}^T = \mathbf{B}\), so:

\[(\mathbf{B} + \mathbf{B}^T)\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{B} + \mathbf{B})\mathbf{x} = 2\mathbf{B}\mathbf{x}\]

This gives the same result as part (b), confirming both methods are equivalent. ✓

Problem 7

Given \(g(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{b}^T\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}\) where: \[\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \\ 3 & -1 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix}\]

a) Dimension of \(g(\mathbf{x})\):

  • \(\mathbf{b}^T\): \(1 \times 3\)
  • \(\mathbf{A}\): \(3 \times 3\)
  • \(\mathbf{x}\): \(3 \times 1\)

Result: \((1 \times 3)(3 \times 3)(3 \times 1) = 1 \times 1\)

Therefore, \(g(\mathbf{x})\) is a scalar.

b) Find \(\frac{\partial g}{\partial \mathbf{x}}\) using the rule:

The rule states: \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{b}^T\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{A}^T\mathbf{b}\)

First, find \(\mathbf{A}^T\): \[\mathbf{A}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\]

Then calculate \(\mathbf{A}^T\mathbf{b}\): \[\frac{\partial g}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{A}^T\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 - 3 + 0 \\ 0 + 1 + 6 \\ 4 - 1 + 12 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 7 \\ 15 \end{bmatrix}\]

c) Calculate \(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}\) first, then expand:

\[\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \\ 3 & -1 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 + 2x_3 \\ 3x_1 - x_2 + x_3 \\ 2x_2 + 4x_3 \end{bmatrix}\]

Then calculate \(\mathbf{b}^T(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x})\): \[g(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 + 2x_3 \\ 3x_1 - x_2 + x_3 \\ 2x_2 + 4x_3 \end{bmatrix}\]

\[= 2(x_1 + 2x_3) - 1(3x_1 - x_2 + x_3) + 3(2x_2 + 4x_3)\]

\[= 2x_1 + 4x_3 - 3x_1 + x_2 - x_3 + 6x_2 + 12x_3\]

\[= -x_1 + 7x_2 + 15x_3\]

d) Verify by taking partial derivatives:

From the scalar form \(g(\mathbf{x}) = -x_1 + 7x_2 + 15x_3\):

\[\frac{\partial g}{\partial x_1} = -1, \quad \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_2} = 7, \quad \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_3} = 15\]

Therefore: \(\frac{\partial g}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 7 \\ 15 \end{bmatrix}\)

This matches our result from part (b). ✓

Bonus Challenge

For the expression \(SSE = (\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})^T(\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})\), let \(\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}\), so \(SSE = \mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{u}\).

To find \(\frac{\partial SSE}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}\), we need:

  1. Chain rule: Since \(SSE = f(\mathbf{u})\) where \(\mathbf{u} = g(\boldsymbol{\beta})\), we need \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{u}} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}\)

  2. Quadratic form derivative: \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{u}) = 2\mathbf{u}\) (since the identity matrix is symmetric)

  3. Linear form derivative: \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}(\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}) = -\mathbf{X}\) (using \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{x}}(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{A}^T\) with \(\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{X}\))

  4. Matrix multiplication for chain rule: Combining the results using appropriate matrix dimensions

The complete solution would involve: - Expanding the quadratic form - Applying the derivative rules for \(\mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{x}\), \(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}\), and \(\mathbf{b}^T\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}\) forms - Using properties of transposes and matrix multiplication

This connects directly to Problems 4, 5, 6, and 7 from this problem set!
